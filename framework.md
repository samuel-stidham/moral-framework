# My Moral Framework - Version 2.0.2

## Preamble

This framework is my personal map of what justice, fairness, and ethical living mean to me. It’s the second version of something I started a while ago — and it represents real growth. Much of that growth came from my work in _PHL-260: Ethical Problem-Solving_ at Southern New Hampshire University.

When I first created this, I was guided mostly by instinct — by what _felt_ right or wrong. But when I started studying ethical theories in depth and analyzed cases like the “Right to Be Forgotten,” I realized that many of my own ideas already reflected principles from these long-established systems. That realization pushed me to rebuild my framework — not to replace what I believe, but to connect my intuition to these time-tested ideas. The goal is to make my moral reasoning stronger, clearer, and more intentional.

Like the first version, this one isn’t meant to be final. It’s a living document — something I expect to keep changing as I keep learning. When I reference other thinkers, it’s not because I agree with everything they say, but because something in their work resonated with me and helped me think more deeply.

I know life doesn’t always line up neatly with theory. Real situations are messy. Values collide. So when I face an ethical conflict, this is the process I plan to follow:

1. **Start with minimizing harm.** I’ll look at the possible outcomes and choose the path that upholds fairness and justice while causing the least harm overall.
2. **Understand the context.** Every situation is unique, and I want to make choices that reflect that reality rather than applying rigid rules.
3. **Reflect and refine.** Afterward, I’ll take time to think about the impact of my decisions — what worked, what didn’t, and how my framework can grow stronger from it.

## Foundations: The Five Ethical Frameworks

In my PHL-260 course, I was introduced to several formal systems for ethical reasoning. As I studied them, I began to see my own intuitive ideas reflected in their structures, and it became clear that a stronger personal framework would need to acknowledge these foundations. Before rebuilding my own principles, I felt it was necessary to outline the core ideas I learned from, both to honor their influence and to clarify the concepts I am drawing upon.

### The Rights Framework

This framework centers on the idea that every person has a set of fundamental rights and an inherent dignity that must be respected. The core question it asks is: "Which option best respects the rights of everyone involved?" It's not about what is legal, but what is morally required to protect an individual's ability to make their own choices about their life, such as the right to privacy, truth, and autonomy. An action that violates these moral rights is considered ethically wrong, even if it might lead to a good outcome for many (SNHU, 2025; Velasquez et al., n.d.).

### The Virtue Framework

Instead of focusing on specific actions, the virtue framework asks a different question: "What kind of person do I want to be?" It argues that ethical action flows from a virtuous character. Virtues are positive habits like honesty, courage, compassion, and integrity, which are developed over time within a community. This approach suggests that the right thing to do is whatever a fully virtuous person would do in that situation, pushing us to act in alignment with our highest ideals (SNHU, 2025; Velasquez et al., n.d.).

### The Utilitarian Framework

This framework is all about the consequences. Its main goal is to produce the most good and the least harm for the greatest number of people. When faced with a decision, a utilitarian approach involves tallying up all the potential positive and negative outcomes for everyone affected. The most ethical choice is the one that creates the best overall balance of good over harm. It's a pragmatic approach that forces a consideration beyond just one's own self-interest (SNHU, 2025; Velasquez et al., n.d.).

### The Care Ethics Framework

Care ethics shifts the focus to our relationships and responsibilities. It suggests that our primary moral duty is to care for the well-being of those with whom we have a concrete relationship—family, friends, and community members. It values empathy and compassion over abstract rules and argues that the context of a situation is critically important. The core question here is: "What do my responsibilities to others require of me in this specific situation?" (SNHU, 2025; Sander-Staudt, n.d.).

### The Justice Framework

This framework taps into that fundamental human sense of fairness, a concept famously explored by philosopher John Rawls in his work _A Theory of Justice_. At its heart, it's based on the principle that people should be treated equally, unless there are ethically relevant differences between them. It challenges me to look closely at how the benefits (the good stuff) and the burdens (the bad stuff) are spread around in any situation, and to push for systems and decisions that are truly impartial and non-discriminatory (Rawls, 1971; SNHU, 2025; Velasquez et al., n.d.).

To figure out what is truly fair, Rawls gave us a powerful thought experiment called the "veil of ignorance." He asks us to imagine designing the rules of a society without knowing who we'll be—we don't know if we'll be rich or poor, a man or a woman, part of the majority or a marginalized group. By stepping behind this "veil," we are forced to create the fairest possible rules because we have to consider what life would be like from the most vulnerable position. It makes me ask, "If I didn't know my own place in the world, what kind of society would I feel safe entering?" (Rawls, 1971; SNHU, 2025; Velasquez et al., n.d.).

## My Guiding Principles: A Synthesis

The principles that follow are the rebuilt, V2.0.2 of my ethical framework. They are my attempt to synthesize my personal intuition with the insights of the formal frameworks I have studied. Each principle builds on the one before it, starting with the bedrock of justice and moving toward its application in the real world.

### 1. Justice Through the Veil of Ignorance

The cornerstone of my ethical framework is an unwavering commitment to justice and fairness. This principle is a synthesis of three foundational ideas: the **Rights Framework** and the broader philosophy of **Humanism** (Law, 2011), which provide the _what_—the inherent dignity and rights of every individual, as articulated in documents like the **United Nations' Universal Declaration of Human Rights** (United Nations, 1948)—and the **Justice Framework**, which provides the _how_—the method for ensuring those rights are upheld fairly and impartially.

My commitment is to view every ethical dilemma through the lens of John Rawls' "veil of ignorance." Before making a decision that affects others, I will ask myself: "If I did not know who I would be in this situation—if I could be the most vulnerable person affected by this choice—what would I consider to be the fairest outcome?" This mindset is my primary tool for distributing benefits and burdens equitably and for respecting the fundamental rights and dignity of all individuals involved.

_(Inspired by the Rights Framework, the Justice Framework, Humanism, and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights; Law, 2011; Rawls, 1971; SNHU, 2025; United Nations, 1948; Velasquez et al., n.d.)._

### 2. Compassion in Action: The Responsibility to Minimize Harm

While justice provides the impartial foundation, it must be applied with a deep sense of compassion and responsibility for its real-world consequences. A just outcome that is delivered without empathy can still cause unnecessary harm. This principle, therefore, gives a human face to justice and is a synthesis of two complementary ideas: the **Care Ethics Framework**, which grounds my actions in the context of real relationships, and the **Utilitarian Framework**, which commits me to actively minimizing harm.

The Care Ethics lens reminds me that ethical decisions are not abstract problems. They affect specific people with unique needs and relationships. Therefore, I must always consider the particular context of a situation and my responsibilities to those involved. My responsibility also extends to the consequences of my actions. Guided by a Utilitarian spirit, I am committed to evaluating the potential impact of my choices, seeking the path that alleviates suffering and creates the greatest balance of well-being for the most people.

Ultimately, justice tells me what is _fair_, but this principle of compassionate action guides me in _how_ to enact that fairness in a way that is both humane and constructive.

_(Inspired by the Care Ethics Framework and the Utilitarian Framework; SNHU, 2025; Sander-Staudt, n.d.; Velasquez et al., n.d.)._

### 3. Integrity in Engagement: A Commitment to Virtue

With a foundation of justice and a guide of compassion, this third principle governs the _character_ of my actions and interactions. It is a direct application of the **Virtue Framework**, which shifts the focus from "What is the right action?" to "What kind of person do I want to be?" It is my commitment to engage with the world in a way that reflects the virtues I aim to cultivate.

The core of this principle is **integrity**, which I define as a synthesis of honesty and a refusal to cause harm. This refusal to cause harm is my personal adoption of the Non-Aggression Principle. While the NAP is a foundational concept in libertarian thought, I embrace it not as a political statement, but as a universal ethical boundary for peaceful interaction that complements any philosophy centered on compassion and respect for individual autonomy. It aligns directly with professional standards like the ACM Code of Ethics, which mandates that one must "Avoid harm," setting a clear and unambiguous boundary for all my interactions (Van der Vossen, n.d.).

Beyond simply avoiding harm, this principle calls for **thoughtful engagement**. It is a commitment to approach conversations and disagreements with reason, care, and honesty, rather than emotional reactivity. It means upholding the dignity of others, even when challenging their ideas. Even humor, when used, should serve this virtuous end—to build understanding and diffuse tension, never to demean. Ultimately, this principle ensures that my conduct is a consistent reflection of the person I strive to be.

_(Inspired by the Virtue Framework, the ACM Code of Ethics, and the Non-Aggression Principle; SNHU, 2025; Van der Vossen, n.d.; Velasquez et al., n.d.)._

### 4. The Pursuit of Wisdom: A Commitment to Growth and Truth

The final principle is the engine that drives all the others. Without a clear understanding of the world, justice can be misapplied, compassion can be misguided, and integrity can be unknowingly compromised. This principle is my commitment to the lifelong pursuit of wisdom through critical thinking, intellectual honesty, and a humble willingness to grow. It is another direct application of the **Virtue Framework**, focused on cultivating the essential virtues that make sound judgment possible.

This pursuit begins with a commitment to **rational inquiry**. I will use reason, evidence, and critical thinking as my primary tools for understanding the world and making ethical decisions. Truth is the foundation of justice, and this means I must hold my own beliefs and the actions of others accountable to a high standard of evidence and logical consistency. This includes seeking accuracy and understanding in the study of all cultures, philosophies, and histories—honoring their roots while challenging harmful or inaccurate narratives.

Above all, this principle is an embrace of **continuous learning**. It is the explicit promise that this framework, and my understanding of it, will never be finished. True growth requires confronting uncomfortable truths and being willing to evolve, even if it means admitting I was wrong. This commitment to growth is what keeps the framework alive, ensuring it remains a tool for ethical living, not a set of stagnant rules.

_(Inspired by the Virtue Framework; SNHU, 2025; Velasquez et al., n.d.)._
